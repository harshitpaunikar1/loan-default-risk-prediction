Lending Into the Unknown:
A Credit-Risk Decision System

The hard part of consumer lending is not the population you already understand - it is the applicants who have no credit history and whose risk you have to estimate from everything except their repayment record. A classification model is only useful here if it connects to an approval policy.

Dataset 307,511 applications
Default Rate ~8.1% class imbalance
Target Metric AUC / KS
Decision Output 4 Bands approve → reject
Domain Consumer credit / NBFC

§ 01The Lending Dilemma

Consumer lenders - banks, NBFCs, digital credit platforms - face a version of the same problem every day: an applicant arrives with a credit request, and the lender has to decide whether to approve, reprice, or decline before they know how that person will actually behave. If the lender approves and the borrower defaults, the lender absorbs a loss. If the lender declines and the borrower would have repaid, the lender has forfeited revenue and the applicant has been denied a service. Both mistakes have real costs; neither is avoidable at scale; the question is which type of error the lender can afford more of at a given moment in its credit cycle.

The Home Credit dataset makes this dilemma concrete. The applicants in this dataset are specifically the "thin file" population - people with insufficient or non-existent formal credit history who are underserved by traditional bureau-based scoring. They apply for consumer loans and the lender has to decide using alternative data: income, employment type, asset ownership, family size, loan purpose, contract type, and the applicant's prior behavior in the lender's own system (if any). The dataset is large enough - 307,511 applications - to build a statistically credible model, but the 8.1 percent default rate means the model has to work against class imbalance: for every defaulter, there are approximately eleven non-defaulters who will complicate the decision boundary.

The business objective, stated plainly in the repository brief, is to identify patterns that predict repayment difficulty so the lender can take action: deny, reduce loan amount, or adjust interest rate upward. What that brief does not spell out is the policy structure through which those actions should happen - and without that structure, a model is a prediction engine disconnected from a lending book.

§ 02Data and Feature Logic

Application Table: The Primary Signal Set

The application_data.csv file contains one row per application with 122 features. That is more features than most EDA exercises need, and it is more than most models can use without feature selection. The features split into roughly four interpretive categories.

Demographic and household features - age, gender, family status, number of children, housing situation - are the weakest direct predictors but serve as important interaction partners with income and employment type. A 35-year-old married applicant with stable employment means something different from an otherwise identical applicant who is unemployed; the demographic features don't predict default alone but help the model understand context.

Income and employment features - income amount, income type (working, commercial associate, pensioner, state servant), employment length - are the most direct indicators of repayment capacity. Income amount is particularly important but requires normalization against loan amount: a ₹30,000 income for a ₹100,000 loan is a different risk profile from the same income against a ₹20,000 loan. The debt-to-income ratio constructed from these two fields outperforms either raw feature independently.

Asset and stability features - car ownership, real estate ownership, document submission completeness - are proxy signals for financial stability and behavioral reliability. Applicants who own assets are, on average, more stable; applicants who submit all required documents without prompting tend to have lower default rates, possibly because document completion correlates with financial organization and engagement.

Bureau-external query features - the AMT_REQ_CREDIT_BUREAU_* columns tracking how many times the applicant was queried at various bureaus in the recent past - are high-signal features that the notebook correctly identifies as important. Multiple recent credit inquiries suggest that the applicant is shopping for credit, possibly because previous requests were rejected or because they are in a period of financial stress. However, zero entries dominate these columns, which limits their statistical contribution unless the model handles zero-inflation appropriately.

Previous Application Table: The Behavioral Dimension

The previous_application.csv file links prior credit applications and outcomes for each current applicant. This is the feature source most likely to improve model discrimination, because it contains actual behavioral evidence - not proxy signals - about how this specific person has behaved with credit in the past. Applicants with approved previous loans who completed repayment are categorically different from applicants whose previous loan was refused or cancelled.

Engineering features from the previous application table requires aggregation: each applicant may have multiple prior applications, so the table needs to be rolled up to one row per applicant with features like "number of previous refused applications," "maximum previous loan amount," and "most recent previous outcome." This joins to the primary application table on the applicant ID. The feature engineering step here is where the most credit-risk-specific domain knowledge shows up in the code.

Data Discipline Note

The 122-feature application table contains columns with up to 65 percent missing values. Dropping rows with any missing value would destroy most of the dataset. The notebook handles this through column-level decisions: columns above a missing-value threshold (50 percent is a common cutoff) are dropped entirely; below that threshold, missing values are imputed with median (continuous) or mode (categorical). The specific threshold matters - imputing at 65 percent missingness introduces substantial noise. The notebook's choice to drop columns above approximately 50 percent is defensible.

§ 03Validation and What the Metrics Actually Mean

Two metrics dominate the evaluation of credit scoring models: AUC-ROC and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic. Both measure rank-ordering ability - how well the model separates defaulters from non-defaulters - but they measure slightly different aspects of that separation and neither is a substitute for the other.

AUC-ROC: The Rank Discrimination Summary

The Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve measures the probability that the model gives a randomly selected defaulter a higher risk score than a randomly selected non-defaulter. An AUC of 0.5 is chance; AUC of 1.0 is perfect separation; in consumer credit, an AUC between 0.70 and 0.80 is considered acceptable for a thin-file population without bureau scores, and above 0.80 is considered good.

The ROC curve itself plots the true positive rate (sensitivity - the fraction of defaulters correctly flagged) against the false positive rate (1 − specificity - the fraction of non-defaulters incorrectly flagged) as the decision threshold varies. Moving along the curve from right to left corresponds to raising the score threshold for labeling an application as risky: the model becomes more selective, catches fewer defaulters, but also incorrectly flags fewer good borrowers. The operating point on this curve is a business decision, not a statistical one - it depends on how the lender weighs lost revenue from declined good borrowers against credit losses from approved defaulters.

KS Statistic: The Maximum Separation

The Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic is the maximum vertical distance between the cumulative distribution function of scores for defaulters and the CDF for non-defaulters. In plain language: if you rank all applicants by risk score from highest to lowest, the KS statistic measures how cleanly the defaulters cluster at the top of that ranking before the non-defaulters appear. A KS of 0 means the distributions are identical; a KS of 1 means perfect separation. In practice, consumer credit models for thin-file populations target KS values in the 30-50 percent range.

KS is particularly useful for scoreband construction because it identifies the score range where discrimination is sharpest - the sweet spot for the approve/review boundary. A model with high AUC but low KS has broad discriminating power but no single score threshold that cleanly separates risk tiers; a model with lower AUC but higher KS may be more useful for policy because the clean separation zone is well-defined even if overall rank ordering is imperfect.

§ 04Scoreband Construction

A model that outputs a probability of default is not yet an approval policy. The conversion from probability to policy requires score transformation and band definition - a step that involves explicit choices about risk tolerance that the data alone cannot make.

From Probability to Score

The raw model output is a probability between 0 and 1, where higher probability = higher estimated default risk. For policy purposes, it is conventional to invert and rescale this to a score where higher = less risky, which aligns with the credit bureau convention that higher scores mean better creditworthiness. A common transformation scales the log-odds of non-default to a 300-900 integer range:

import numpy as np

def prob_to_score(p_default, base_score=600, pdo=50, base_odds=1.0):
    """
    Convert default probability to a scorecard-style integer score.
    pdo: Points to Double Odds - score increment that halves default risk.
    base_score: score at which odds = base_odds (e.g., 1:1 odds = 600)
    """
    # Avoid log(0) - clip extreme probabilities
    p_default = np.clip(p_default, 1e-6, 1 - 1e-6)
    odds_good = (1 - p_default) / p_default       # odds of non-default
    log_odds   = np.log(odds_good)
    factor     = pdo / np.log(2)                  # score per log-odds unit
    score = base_score + factor * (log_odds - np.log(base_odds))
    return np.round(score).astype(int)

Band Cutoffs: Not a Statistical Question

The cutoffs that separate Band A from Band B from Band C from Band D are not discovered by the model. They are set by the credit policy team based on three inputs: the estimated default rate in each score range (derived from the validation set), the lender's current target portfolio default rate (a function of capital adequacy and risk appetite), and the expected volume at each band (how many applications fall in each zone at the proposed cutoffs).

A lender that has been running above its target NPA ratio will set stricter cutoffs, declining more applications and accepting lower origination volume in exchange for better portfolio quality. A lender that is growing aggressively may set more permissive cutoffs, accepting more Band C applications into the review queue to maintain volume. The model doesn't make this call; the model gives the credit policy team the empirical basis to make it.

§ 05Handling Class Imbalance: The 8.1% Problem

With only 8.1 percent of applications being actual defaulters, a model that predicts "no default" for every application achieves 91.9 percent accuracy while being useless for the purpose it was built for. Accuracy is the wrong optimization target for this problem; discrimination is what matters.

Three strategies handle the imbalance in practice:

  • Class weights. Penalize the model more for missing a default than for a false alarm. Scikit-learn's classifiers accept a class_weight parameter; setting it proportionally to the inverse of class frequency (approximately 1:11 here) forces the model to work harder to correctly rank defaulters. This preserves all training data and is the most common approach.
  • SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling). Generate synthetic defaulter observations by interpolating between existing defaulters in feature space. This can help tree-based models learn better decision boundaries in sparse default zones, but it can also introduce noise if defaulter feature space has complex boundaries. Use only in training; never on validation or test sets.
  • Threshold tuning post-training. Train on the imbalanced data but adjust the classification threshold (default 0.5) downward so more applicants are flagged as risky. This shifts the precision-recall tradeoff: the lender catches more defaulters but also declines more good applicants. Where on the precision-recall curve the lender should operate is - again - a business decision, not a model decision.

§ 06Connecting Output to Approval and Monitoring Policy

The credit model's output is only valuable if it is embedded in a decision workflow that uses it consistently, updates it regularly, and monitors its performance against actual outcomes.

Approval Policy

The approval ladder in Fig. 1 shows the intended routing. In a production system, this means that every application flows through a scoring API before reaching an underwriter. Band A and Band B decisions are automated; Band C applications route to a human underwriter with the score, the key driver features, and the relevant policy thresholds visible in the underwriting interface. Band D applications receive an automated decline with a reason code - not "your score was low" but a specific reason code mapped to the highest-weight negative features (e.g., "insufficient employment stability," "debt-to-income ratio exceeds threshold"), which is required for regulatory compliance in most jurisdictions.

Monitoring Policy

A credit model's performance degrades over time as the population shifts - new customer segments enter, economic conditions change, the lender's own marketing changes who applies. The standard monitoring framework tracks three things: (1) the score distribution of new applications versus the training cohort (a Population Stability Index above 0.25 is a signal to retrain); (2) the actual default rate in each band against the expected default rate (a divergence of more than 30 percent relative is a red flag); and (3) feature drift in the top-10 predictive features (any feature whose distribution has shifted significantly is a candidate for investigation or model update).

Model retraining is not a one-time event. For a lender processing thousands of applications per month, a model trained on historical data from more than 12-18 months ago is likely degraded to some degree. The pipeline to retrain on fresh labeled data (where "labeled" means applications old enough that their repayment outcome is known) needs to exist as a production process, not as a one-off notebook run.

§ 07What the Model Cannot Do

The model predicts default probability based on application-time features. It does not see everything that matters.

It does not see job loss after approval. An applicant who scores Band A in January and loses their job in March is now a default risk regardless of their application-time score. Income protection products, disbursement-time employment verification, and post-disbursement monitoring (looking for early payment behavior signals) handle the gap the application-time model cannot see.

It does not see fraud. A well-constructed synthetic identity (a "sleeper" application built with a clean fabricated credit profile) will score favorably and default by design. Fraud detection requires a separate system - behavioral biometrics, device fingerprinting, anomaly detection on application patterns - that runs in parallel to the credit model, not as a component of it.

It does not adjudicate fairness. A model trained on historical approval data inherits the biases embedded in that history. If the lender historically approved women at lower rates than men with similar profiles, the model will learn to score women lower, amplifying the original bias. Fair lending compliance requires disparate impact testing across protected characteristics, with remediation through constrained optimization or post-processing adjustments if disparate impact is detected. This is not optional for regulated lenders - it is a legal obligation.

A credit scoring model is a disciplined way of ranking applicants by estimated default risk. It is subordinate to the lending policy, not a replacement for it. The policy decisions - who to approve, at what rate, with what collections response - are institutional choices that the model informs but never makes autonomously. The value of the model lies in making those policy choices more consistent and more empirically grounded than individual underwriter judgment can reliably be at scale.
